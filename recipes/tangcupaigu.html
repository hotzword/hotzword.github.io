Sweet and sour pork rib | 糖醋排骨

Sweet and sour pork rib is one of the most popular dishes all around China. A hundred people might have a hundred ways to cook it. Some approaches might be challenging for beginners -- like melting sugar in the oil with just right heat so the sugar goes brown but does not burn, then heat a large amount of oil and deep fry pork rib so that the rib goes crispy outside and tender inside. This is the most authentic way which you can possibly try in restaurants. But doing so requires an extremely good sense for the timing, as well as a really powerful stove. Here I will introduce an simplified and foolproof version, which comprimises for the perfect brown shiny "sugar color", but maintains the essense of sweet and sour flavor. What's more, substuting deep fry with slow brew makes it healthier for everyday meal.

STEPS

01.

Put pork rib in a pot and add enough cold water to cover the rib. Add cooking wine and sliced ginger to the pot. Heat until boil. Transfer the pork to a colader, wash off the foam in running water and drain the pork rib.

02.

While heating pork, prepare the sauce. In a small bowl, add in two teaspoons of cooking wine, four teaspoons of soy souce, 6 teaspoons of vinigar, and 4 teaspoons of sugar. Boil about 1L water in a kettle.

03.

Use high heat all the time. Heat your wok and add three teaspoons of oil. Add in pork rib and stir for 2 min.

04.

Use medium heat. Add in the pre-mixed sauce, constantly stirring, until the sauce and rib is fully mixed and all liquid is about to turn dry.

05.

Pour in the kettle-boiled water to cover the rib. Cover the wok with a lib. Use high heat to boil the water, then reduce the heat. Simmer for 30 min.

06.

I always get those prok ribs at grocery fresh counters (like whole foods) and ask butcher to chop the ribs into small pieaces. Costco has readily chopped pork ribs as well.

Normally, adding in more oil can make the dish taste better, especially when stir-frying eggs -- another fact generally hold for Sichuan dishes. But too much oil is unhealth and I just can't do it to myself. My mom told me to add oil not at the beginning, but when the wok is heated and starts to give off thin smoke, calling it "hot wok cold oil". I don't know why, perhaps it's good for the oil.

The scallion is only to make the flavor rich, and also add in a pinch of green to give a better look. I often skip that when I feel lazy, it doesn;t hurt the key flavor.

Alright, although I have put down so much words, I hope you find the dish easy enough. If you have succeeded tomato and egg stir-fry, congratulations, you have got the entry ticket to the whole amazing world of stir-fires!
